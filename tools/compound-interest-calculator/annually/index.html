Annual Compound Interest Formula

A = P × (1 + r)^t + C × [(1 + r)^t − 1] / r P = principal · r = annual rate (decimal) · t = years · C = annual contribution CAGR = (A/P)^(1/t) − 1 (reverse calculation)

Example: $10,000 + $2,400/year at 7% for 20 years → Final: $155,120. Of this, $10,000 principal + $48,000 contributions = $58,000 invested; $97,120 is pure compound interest. The Rule of 72 says this doubles every 72÷7 = 10.3 years, so $10,000 alone grows to ~$38,697 in 20 years.

Annual Compound Interest — FAQ

What is annual compounding?

Annual compounding means interest is calculated and added to your balance once per year. After year 1, you earn interest on the original principal. In year 2, you earn interest on principal + year 1 interest. This snowball effect is compound interest. Formula: A = P(1+r)^t. It's simpler than monthly/daily compounding and used for bonds, some CDs, and textbook finance calculations.

What is the Rule of 72?

Quick mental math to estimate doubling time: years = 72 ÷ annual rate. At 6%: 12 years. At 8%: 9 years. At 10%: 7.2 years. At 3% (inflation): 24 years. The rule works because ln(2) ≈ 0.693 ≈ 72% for common interest rates. For precise calculation: years = ln(2)/ln(1+r). At 7%: exactly 10.24 years, vs Rule of 72 estimate of 10.29 years — very close.

How is annual compound interest used for bonds?

Bond yield calculations typically use annual compounding. A $1,000 bond with a 5% coupon reinvested at 5% annually for 10 years: A = 1000 × (1.05)^10 = $1,629. Zero-coupon bonds use annual compounding to calculate present value: if you want $10,000 in 10 years at 5%, the price today = 10,000 / (1.05)^10 = $6,139. The S&P 500's historical CAGR of ~10% means $10,000 becomes $67,275 over 20 years with annual compounding.

What is CAGR and how do I calculate it?

CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate) is annual compounding in reverse: given a start value and end value, what annual rate achieved that growth? Formula: CAGR = (End/Start)^(1/years) − 1. If $10,000 grew to $45,000 in 20 years: CAGR = (45000/10000)^(1/20) − 1 = 7.87%. The S&P 500's 10-year CAGR (2015–2025) was approximately 12–13%. CAGR is used to compare investments with different timeframes.

How does inflation affect annual compound interest?

Inflation erodes purchasing power. Real return = (1 + nominal rate) / (1 + inflation rate) − 1. At 7% return with 3% inflation: real return ≈ 3.88%. $10,000 growing to $38,697 in 20 years at 7% has a real (inflation-adjusted) value of only $21,421 in today's dollars. This is why financial advisors say you need 7-10% annual returns — to significantly outpace inflation.
