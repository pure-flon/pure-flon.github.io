Daily Compound Interest Formula

A = P × (1 + r/365)^(365t) P = principal · r = annual rate (decimal) · t = years APY = (1 + r/365)^365 − 1

Example: $10,000 at 5% APR compounded daily for 10 years → A = 10,000 × (1.05/365 + 1)^3650 = $16,487. The effective APY is 5.127% (vs the stated 5.0% APR). Banks advertise APY to show the actual annual return after compounding — always compare APY, not APR.

Daily Compound Interest — FAQ

What does "compounded daily" mean?

Compounded daily means interest is calculated and added to your balance 365 times per year (once per day). Each day's interest is based on a slightly higher balance than the day before. This creates the "compounding" effect: you earn interest on your interest. Daily compounding is standard for most high-yield savings accounts (Marcus, Ally, SoFi, Discover) and some CDs.

Is daily compounding really better than monthly?

Technically yes, but practically the difference is tiny. On $50,000 at 5% for 30 years: monthly = $224,060; daily = $224,409 — just $349 difference. The interest rate matters enormously more. A 5.1% account compounded monthly beats a 5.0% account compounded daily by $3,200 over 30 years. Focus on finding the best APY, not the compounding frequency.

What is APR vs APY with daily compounding?

APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the stated rate without compounding. APY (Annual Percentage Yield) is the effective rate after compounding. For a 5% APR compounded daily: APY = (1 + 0.05/365)^365 − 1 = 5.127%. When a bank advertises "5.00% APY," they've already calculated the compounding effect — compare APY to APY when shopping savings accounts.

How much interest does $10,000 earn in a year at 5% compounded daily?

$10,000 × 5.127% (effective APY) = $512.67 in the first year. With simple/annual compounding at 5%, you'd earn exactly $500.00. The daily compounding adds $12.67 more. After 10 years, the $10,000 grows to $16,487 with daily compounding vs $16,289 with annual compounding — a $198 difference that grows over time.

Which savings accounts use daily compounding?

Most online high-yield savings accounts compound daily: Marcus by Goldman Sachs, Ally Bank, SoFi, Discover Bank, American Express HYSA, and most credit union savings accounts. Traditional brick-and-mortar banks often compound monthly or quarterly. Check your bank's terms — they must disclose both APR and APY so you can see the compounding effect.
