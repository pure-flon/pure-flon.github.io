Stock Position Size Formula

Dollar Risk = Account Balance × Risk % Risk Per Share = Entry Price − Stop Loss Price Shares = Dollar Risk / Risk Per Share Total Position = Shares × Entry Price Risk:Reward = (Take Profit − Entry) / (Entry − Stop Loss)

Example: $25,000 account, 1% risk = $250. Entry $150, stop $145 = $5 risk/share. Shares = $250 ÷ $5 = 50 shares. Total position = $7,500 (30% of account). If stock hits your stop at $145, you lose exactly $250 = 1%. With take profit at $165 (+$15/share): R:R = $15/$5 = 3:1. Profit if TP hit = 50 × $15 = $750.

Stock Position Sizing — FAQ

Why can't I just buy $X worth of any stock?

Buying a fixed dollar amount ignores volatility and stop-loss distance. A $5,000 position in a stable utility stock with a $2 stop is very different from a $5,000 position in a volatile tech stock with a $15 stop. With risk-based position sizing, your dollar risk is constant ($250 at 1%) regardless of the stock — so 20 consecutive losses costs you 20% of account, not 20 × $5,000 = $100,000. Fixed-dollar sizing causes account blowups; fixed-risk sizing preserves capital.

What is the 1% rule in trading?

The 1% rule means never risk more than 1% of account on a single trade. It was popularized by market wizard traders (Van Tharp, William O'Neil). At 1% risk: you need 100 consecutive losing trades to lose 63% of account. That's a 100-trade streak that's only possible if your strategy is fundamentally broken. The rule prevents "death by 1,000 cuts" and allows you to trade through normal losing streaks (typically 5-15 in a row) without major damage.

How do I use this calculator for ETFs like SPY or QQQ?

Same formula applies. Entry: current ETF price. Stop: below key support, moving average, or 1-2% below entry. Example: SPY at $500, stop at $490 ($10 risk). With $50,000 account at 1% risk ($500 max loss): shares = $500 / $10 = 50 shares of SPY. Position = $25,000. ETFs are less volatile than individual stocks, so stops can often be tighter, allowing larger share counts. Avoid trading SPY options with this calculator — options sizing requires different formulas.

How many stocks can I trade with a $10,000 account?

With $10,000 and 1% risk ($100/trade): if each position averages $1,000-2,000 (10-20% of account), you can hold 5-10 positions simultaneously. At $2 risk/share, you'd buy 50 shares at $20-40/share stocks. Concentration in fewer, higher-conviction trades is usually better than spreading thin across 20+ positions. Commissions matter more at small account sizes — use commission-free brokers (Robinhood, Webull, Fidelity) so a $100 position isn't eaten by $10 in fees.
